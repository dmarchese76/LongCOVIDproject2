Where Have All the Long COVID Clinics Gone?

A nationwide survey reveals the uncertain state of specialized care for Americans living with the chronic condition

Delfina Marchese |

When the University of North Carolina announced it closing last June, it left thousands of patients without specialized care. Since its launch in 2021, the clinic has seen over 3500 patients from over 20 states.

An unpublished survey shared with the Sick Times revealed the severity of their conditions: 43% of patients reported stopping work, 33% went into debt, and 53% had difficulty with activities of daily living.

“The clinic closing sends me into a panic,” said Gillian Lizars, a patient of the clinic at the time. “I think to myself, ‘Oh my God, no help is coming.’”

UNC wasn’t alone.

In 2022, 426 hospitals and clinics across the United States claimed to offer Long COVID care, according to Science News.

Each dot on this map represents one of them, a sign that specialized care was widely available in the aftermath of the pandemic.

Three years later, the Sick Times sent a survey to all of them to find out what type of care they offer now.

Only 27 answered our questions.

Nearly 400 past providers never replied to our follow-up calls or emails, raising questions about whether these clinics still provide care.

Of the 27 that did respond, only 22 said they still treat Long COVID patients.

More clinics may offer care, but the paltry response to our survey suggests that patients seeking treatment may struggle like we did to find providers.

Some clinics go beyond just treating Long COVID - they provide care navigators and health coaches who help patients manage referrals to cardiologists and neurologists, plus handle the paperwork for disability applications. These programs represent a comprehensive and robust type of Long COVID Care in the U.S.

Others, focuses solely on rehabilitation services. These types of programs help patients manage symptoms like fatigue, breathing difficulties, and mobility issues, often through physical therapy.

But rehabilitation alone can’t replace the multidisciplinary care many patients once received, highlighting the lack of comprehensive care for people suffering from Long COVID.

Clinics shut down, gone silent or even scaled back. Patients didn’t dissapear, care did. Its not progress is abandonment.
